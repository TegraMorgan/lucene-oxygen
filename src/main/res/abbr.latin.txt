<p>This is a <b>list of common <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">abbreviations</a></b>. Nearly all the abbreviations below have been adopted by <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a>. However, with some exceptions (for example, <i>versus</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Modus_operandi" title="Modus operandi">modus operandi</a></i>), most of the Latin referent words and phrases are still foreign and unknown to English. In a few cases, English referents have replaced the original Latin ones (e.g., "<a href="/wiki/Rest_in_peace" title="Rest in peace">rest in peace</a>" for R.I.P. and "post script" for P.S.).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> was once the universal academic language in Europe. From the 18th century authors started using their mother tongues to write books, papers or proceedings. Even when Latin fell out of use, many Latin abbreviations continued to be used due to their precise simplicity and Latin's status as a learnèd language.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In July 2016, the government of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> announced that its websites would no longer use Latin abbreviations.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<td><span id="ceteris_paribus"></span>Cp</td>
<td><i><a href="/wiki/Ceteris_paribus" title="Ceteris paribus">ceteris paribus</a></i></td>
<td>"all other things being equal"</td>
<td>Commonly used in economics, ceteris paribus allows for supply and demand models to reflect specific variables. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Less_common_abbreviations_and_usages">Less common abbreviations and usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Latin_abbreviations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Less common abbreviations and usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li>A = amen</li>

<li>AMDG (<i><a href="/wiki/Ad_maiorem_Dei_gloriam" title="Ad maiorem Dei gloriam">Ad maiorem Dei gloriam</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_majorem_Dei_gloriam" class="mw-redirect" title="Ad majorem Dei gloriam">ad majorem Dei gloriam</a></i>): Latin - "For the greater glory of God". It is the <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Society of Jesus</a>.</li>
<li>a.u. (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anno_urbis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anno urbis (page does not exist)">anno urbis</a></i>): Latin for "The year of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">the city</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-AllAcronyms_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllAcronyms-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>a.U.c. (<i><a href="/wiki/Ab_Urbe_condita" class="mw-redirect" title="Ab Urbe condita">ab Urbe condita</a></i> or <i>Anno Urbis conditae</i>): Latin for "from the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">the City</a>":<sup id="cite_ref-USGPOstyle_2-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGPOstyle-2">[2]</a></sup> it refers to the <a href="/wiki/Founding_of_Rome" title="Founding of Rome">founding of Rome</a>, which occurred in <a href="/wiki/750s_BC" title="750s BC">753 BC</a> according to <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a>'s count. Used as a reference point in ancient Rome for establishing dates, before being supplanted by other systems. Also <i>anno Urbis conditae</i> (a.U.c.) ("in the year that the City [Rome] was founded"). For example, the year 2007 <a href="/wiki/AD" class="mw-redirect" title="AD">AD</a> is the year 2761 <i>ab Urbe condita</i> (753 + 1 + 2007 = 2761); though, rigorously speaking, the year <i>a.U.c.</i> begins on April 21, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birthday_of_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Birthday of Rome (page does not exist)">birthday of Rome</a></i> (i.e. the day that <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">Romulus</a> was traditionally believed to have founded the Eternal City). (The reason for adding 1 to 753 is that the Romans counted dates "inclusively," i.e., including both the first and the last day or year in the count.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">A.B.</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Artium_Baccalaureus" class="mw-redirect" title="Artium Baccalaureus">Artium Baccalaureus</a></i>), "<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a>, BA, or AB), is an undergraduate bachelor's degree awarded for either a course or a program in the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a> or the sciences, or both.</li>

(<i>et sequentes</i>, or <i>et sequentia</i>): "and the following" (use <i>et seqq or et sequa.</i> if "the following" is plural).<sup id="cite_ref-USGPOstyle_2-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGPOstyle-2">[2]</a></sup> Not unlike the full colon [&nbsp;: ] which means "the following" i.e. that which follows is a listing of that which precedes the '&nbsp;: '. <li>S.D.X (<i>Sanctus Dominus Christus</i>): "Holy Lord Christ"<sup id="cite_ref-Worley_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Worley-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>S.D.I.X (<i>Salvator Dominus Iesus Christus</i>): "Saviour Lord Jesus Christ"<sup id="cite_ref-Worley_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Worley-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>S.J.D. (<i>Scientiae Juridicae Doctor</i>) or J.S.D. (<i>Juridicae Scientiae Doctor</i>), "<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Juridical_Science" title="Doctor of Juridical Science">Doctor of Juridical Science</a>"</li>
<li>Sc.D. (<i>Scientiae Doctor</i>) or D.Sc. (<i>Doctor Scientiae</i>), "<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a>"</li>
<li>sphalm. (<i>sphalma typographicum</i>): a misprint.</li>
<li>S.P.D. (<i>salutem plurimam dicit</i>), sends many greetings, e.g. Areia S.P.D. Apollonio. (Areia sends many greetings to Apollonius)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>S.P.Q.R. (<i><a href="/wiki/Senatus_Populusque_Romanus" class="mw-redirect" title="Senatus Populusque Romanus">Senatus Populusque Romanus</a></i>): "Senate and People of Rome"</li>
<li>sqq. (<i><a href="/wiki/Sequentia" class="mw-redirect" title="Sequentia">sequentia</a></i>): "the following ones": same as "ff", used for an unspecified number of pages following the one cited.</li>
<li>S.T.T.L. (<i><a href="/wiki/Sit_tibi_terra_levis" title="Sit tibi terra levis">sit tibi terra levis</a></i>) means "May the earth rest lightly on you" and was used in similar manner to R.I.P.</li>
<li>s.v. (<i><a href="/wiki/Sub_verbo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sub verbo">sub verbo</a></i>): "Under the word or heading", as in a dictionary</li>
<li>S.V.B.E.E.V. (<i>si vales bene est ego valeo</i>): "if you are well, it is good. I am well." Among the Romans, this was a traditional salutation at the beginning of a letter.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Th.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Th.D.">Th.D.</a> (<i>Theologiae Doctor</i>): "<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Theology" title="Doctor of Theology">Doctor of Theology</a>"</li>
<li>ult. (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultimo_mense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ultimo mense (page does not exist)">ultimo mense</a></i>): "last month" (see also <i>inst.</i> and <i>prox.</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-USGPOstyle_2-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGPOstyle-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>u.s. (<i><a href="/wiki/Ut_supra" class="mw-redirect" title="Ut supra">ut supra</a></i>): "as above".</li>
<li>V.C. (<i><a href="/wiki/Vi_coactus" title="Vi coactus">vi coactus</a></i>): "constrained by force". Used when forced to sign ("or else&nbsp;...")</li>
<li>V.I. (<i>Venerate Iesum</i>): "Venerate Jesus"<sup id="cite_ref-Worley_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Worley-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>v.i. (<i><a href="/wiki/Vide_infra" class="mw-redirect" title="Vide infra">vide infra</a></i>) means "see below".</li>
<li>v.s. (<i><a href="/wiki/Vide_supra" class="mw-redirect" title="Vide supra">vide supra</a></i>) means "see above".</li>
<li>X (Christus): "<a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a>"</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Used_in_biology">Used in biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Latin_abbreviations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Used in biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aff." class="mw-redirect" title="Aff.">aff.</a> (<i>affinis</i>): having affinity with, similar but not identical to</li>
<li>auct. (<i><a href="/wiki/Auctorum" title="Auctorum">auctorum</a></i>): of the authors, i.e. written like that by various authors</li>
<li>Ca. (<a href="/wiki/Candidatus" title="Candidatus">Candidatus</a>): for candidate names of organisms that have not been completely accepted</li>
<li>ex. (<i>exemplar</i>): example or specimen; plural abbreviated as exx.</li>
<li>f. sp. (<i><a href="/wiki/Forma_specialis" title="Forma specialis">forma specialis</a></i>): a special form adapted to a specific host; plural abbreviated as ff. spp.</li>
<li>in coll. (<i>in collectionem</i>): in the collection, often followed by the name of a collection or museum</li>
<li>indet. (<i>indeterminans</i>): undetermined, unidentified</li>
<li>leg. (<i>legit</i>): he or she collected, often followed by the name of the collector</li>
<li>nob. (<i>nobis</i>): by us, used to indicate that the writer(s) are the author(s) of a scientific name</li>
<li>sp. (<i><a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a></i>): species (singular); plural also <i>species</i>, abbreviated as spp.</li>
<li>sp. nov. (<i><a href="/wiki/Species_nova" title="Species nova">species nova</a></i>): new species (singular); plural is <i>species novae</i>, abbreviated as spp. nov.</li>
<li>ssp. (<i><a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a></i>): subspecies (singular); plural is <i>subspecies</i>, abbreviated sspp.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inter_alios" class="mw-redirect" title="Inter alios">inter alios</a>: Latin for "among others", i.e. people, or legal entities, but not an abbreviation.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a>, Latin for "thus", but not an abbreviation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stet" title="Stet">stet</a>, Latin for "let it stand", but not an abbreviation. Used in editing to indicate that something should remain as it is, and not be changed.</li>
